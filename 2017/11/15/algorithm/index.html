Stable Matching

Consider a set M={m1,…mn} of n men, and a set W={w1,….,wn}
What is perfect Matching S’?
Consider N*M, each of n men is matched with just one woman and each of n women is matched with just one man. And everyone exists only exactly once in S’.
What is Stable?
Consider woman w1 is matched with man m1, after that man m2 proposed to w1, and w1 really prefer m2 to m1. Then w1 will match with m2 and reject m1. However, m2 find w2 later, which he prefers to w1. Then m2 will match with w2 and reject w1. Everything is such a mass! So Gale and Shapley defined a stable matching:
if w1 and m1 is matched, then w1 prefers m1 to other men, or other men prefers his current partner over w1.
What is instability?
If we have two pairs (m,w) and (m’,w’) in S, with the property that m prefers w’ to w, and w’ prefers m to m’, then we say that pair(m,w’) is unstable. Because nothing can stop them from abandoning their current partners and match with each other.

Now, we add the notion of preference, each man has a preference list for all women, and each woman has a preference list for all men.
Given those preference lists from women and men, how to find a stable match for each woman and each man?
Here is the Gale-Shapley algorithm:

Start with empty matching:e
While there exists a free man:
    Choose such a man m, and let him propose to the woman w, which has not proposed and 
    highest-ranked in m's preference list. 
    If w is free, then (m,w) is matched.
    Else if w prefers m to her current partner m',
                   then (m,w) is matched and m' becomes free.
            Else m remains free.
            Endif
    Endif
 EndWhile

Analyze the Algorithm:

  1. The G-S algorithm terminates after at most $n^2$ iterations of the While loop.
    In each iteration, man only propose to woman he has never proposed before, and there are only $n^2$ pairs of men and women in total, so that the iterations can be at most $n^2$ times. And after all iterations, there can’t be any free man.
    Proof: If there exists a man m, he has already proposed to every woman and he is still free. Which means all women has rejected him and was matched with someone. There must be n engaged men at this point, but there are only n men total, and m is not engaged, so this is a contradiction.
  2. We need to prove the correctness of this algorithm.
    Assume for contradiction:
    As defined early, we have two pairs (m,w) and (m’,w’) in S, with the property that m prefers w’ to w, and w’ prefers m to m’. In the execution of algorithm that produced S, m proposed to w’ first, after he was rejected by w’ then he proposed to w. Which means w’ is matched with someone m’’, who ranks higher than m in w’s preference list. However, in the end w’ is matched with m’, which ranks lower than m in w’s preference list. This is a contradiction. (Because women only accept those who ranks higher than her current partner)
